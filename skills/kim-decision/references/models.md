# Models

Use abstract models.

Do not write named-person thinking.

Use concrete names only as evidence in the final answer.

## Essence model

Find the controlling condition.

Questions:

- What must be true?
- What does the subject actually need?
- What condition breaks the plan?

Output:

```text
Finding:
Fix:
```

## Path model

Find where movement stops.

Questions:

- Where does the subject stop?
- What is misunderstood?
- Which action is too hard?

## Constraint model

Find the hard boundary.

Questions:

- What is limited?
- What cannot be changed?
- What rule applies?

## Evidence model

Find what must be checked.

Questions:

- What is known?
- What is assumed?
- What is missing?
- What needs a source?

## Incentive model

Find motivation mismatch.

Questions:

- Why does the subject act?
- Why does the provider act?
- Why does the channel help?
- Where do incentives conflict?

## Friction model

Find effort cost.

Questions:

- What takes too many steps?
- What requires too much trust?
- What forces too many decisions?

## Probability model

Find the base rate.

Questions:

- How do similar attempts usually fail?
- What evidence changes the odds?
- What should be expected by default?

## Risk model

Find downside.

Questions:

- What fails first?
- What costs the most if wrong?
- What must be protected?

## Feedback model

Find the loop.

Questions:

- What signal is captured?
- What changes after the signal?
- How does the next attempt improve?

## Compounding model

Find reusable value.

Questions:

- What becomes an asset?
- What can be reused?
- What makes the next attempt cheaper or better?

## Boundary model

Find the hard line.

Questions:

- What cannot be done?
- What claim needs proof?
- What record must be kept?

## Narrative model

Find transmission.

Questions:

- Can the subject explain it in one sentence?
- What example makes it clear?
- What line should be remembered?

## Sharp kernel model (锋利内核模型)

Find the smallest sentence that keeps the decision sharp.

Use when the answer risks becoming broad, polite, or complete but not forceful.

Questions:

- What is the one claim the user must remember?
- Which subject, pain, offer, proof, or threshold makes the claim specific?
- What obvious but weaker path should be rejected?
- What first move proves the kernel is real?

Output:

```text
Kernel:
Reject:
Proof move:
```

## Selection rule

Use one model for simple tasks.

Use at least three models for complex tasks.
